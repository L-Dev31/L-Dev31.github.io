The Brief

OG FM is an underground French webradio most people have never heard of. That’s the point. Their motto: “Pour les jeunes cons qui sont devenus vieux.” For the young fools who grew old. A station for people who refuse to let the music they loved quietly disappear. For their 30th anniversary, they needed something to match that energy. The ask: a complete brand identity system (logo mark + logotype, brand charter, 3 print executions, 1 social media asset), built for a station with near-zero visual presence and a fiercely niche, nostalgia-driven audience. The identity had to feel urgent, physical, unapologetic. Something that couldn’t be mistaken for any other webradio.

Punk isn’t the absence of a system. It’s a system that refuses everyone else’s.

The Problem

Invisible Brand, Loud Station

Their original logo looked like every other webradio. Generic, balanced, forgettable. For a station built on cultural defiance, the visual identity was its biggest contradiction. OG FM had a voice. It just didn’t have a face. The new identity needed to be immediately recognizable, impossible to confuse with anyone else, and defensible across every format it would touch: social media, posters, print, anniversary campaign materials.

The Process

1. Strategic Foundation: Aesthetic Direction

Research pointed straight to UK DIY and fanzine culture. The right direction, with one problem: every contemporary brand trying to look punk borrows that aesthetic in Photoshop, and it shows. So the real question wasn’t what to reference. It was how to make the result feel genuinely earned rather than stylistically borrowed. That distinction became the whole strategy: if the process was authentic, the output couldn’t be faked.

2. Logo Development: Analog-First Execution

The logo came from a ballpoint pen and a sheet of aged paper. No grid, no undo, no vector smoothing. Every mark permanent from the moment it touched the page. Once scanned, Illustrator’s job wasn’t to clean anything up. It was to lock the chaos in place: vectorizing trembling lines raw, preserving every irregularity as a deliberate feature rather than correcting it. The result was a mark that software alone couldn’t have produced, and that no one could mistake for something generated by a template.

3. Type System & Brand Charter

Seven typefaces, each chosen to carry a different era: punk, 80s rock, DIY fanzines, contemporary underground. Not chosen to look interesting. Chosen because together they told the full history of what OG FM actually is. The system wasn’t about breaking rules for shock value. It was about building new ones: size hierarchies, spacing ratios, stretch and compress parameters that forced seven competing fonts into coherent coexistence. That system held across 30+ applications (posters, social posts, print materials) without any two assets feeling like copies of each other. The brand charter locked it all in: logo variations (horizontal, stacked, icon-only), a 5-color palette, the full type system, spacing grid, and usage guidelines.

4. Campaign Execution & Deliverables

Five deliverables for the 30th anniversary campaign: an Instagram post, an A2 poster, an A4 flyer, a merchandise label, and a 12-page brand charter. Each different in format. Identical in DNA. The test of any identity system isn’t how it looks on a single poster. It’s whether the rules survive contact with every format they touch, applied consistently without losing tension. Logo, typography, color, grid: all held. The underlying rules directed every decision without making any two pieces look like copies. The system worked.

A strong identity isn’t beautiful. It’s immediately recognizable. And impossible to ignore.

What This Built

First complete brand identity. First graphic charter. Built solo, from brief to final assets in Adobe Illustrator: logo system, type system, 5-color palette, 3 print executions, 1 social media asset, 12-page brand charter. What it proved: constraint-based thinking doesn’t kill creative work. It’s what makes creative work scale. A design system isn’t a cage. It’s the thing that makes every future decision faster, cleaner, and harder to get wrong.
